    Practical Computer Security

    Familiar Tips

    1. Keep your software up-to-date ("patched" with automatic-updates)
    2. Use anti-virus software
    3. Don't open email attachments from people you don't recognize
    4. Don't click on links that go to strange URLs
    5. Don't use your laptop for important things on open networks
    6. Don't share passwords across different sites
    7. Password protect your phone and encrypt your files
    8. Use passwords that are hard to break
    9. Use a password management program
    10. Configure your firewall correctly

    What they are really saying

    1. Keep your software up-to-date ("patched" with automatic-updates)

      Buy our software even if it is flawed!
      And let us enter your computer the same way bad people do!

    2. Use anti-virus software

      Buy our friend's software!
      And let us keep it up to date by entering the same way bad people do!

    3. Don't open email attachments from people you don't recognize

      Have no new correspondence!
      Have no life!

    4. Don't click on links that go to strange URLs

      This is actually a good one -- I have no complaints

    5. Don't use your laptop for important things on open networks

      Go back to your cubicle to do work!

    6. Password protect your phone and encrypt your files

      Make your phone even harder to use!

    7. Don't share passwords across different sites

      Have lots of passwords so you forget them!

      Use complicated passwords you can't remember!

    9. Use a password management program

    These are all worth heeding, but here are MY suggestions:







    Some Advanced Ideas

    1. Unplug your Ethernet cable now and then when you are not actively uploading or downloading
      • Unplug it at the wall
      • Or better, buy a coupler and a 1m extension cable and unplug it at your physical desktop
      • Even if you are imperfect, it will make many hackers go away
      • They can't sell your machine on a botnet black market if they can't control it
      • If you are down 80% of the time, they need a 5x larger DDOS attack
      • Or do you WANT to be part of someone's botnet?




    2. Move data off your mobile devices often
      • Think of it like the back seat of your car
      • You can put things there, but you can't leave them there
      • Your mobile devices are not your bff's
      • Or do you LIKE losing data?






    3. Use plain text as often as possible
      • Open your email as plain text in a simple mail reader, nothing fancy (learn unix, not angry birds!)
      • Encourage your correspondents to use plain text unless absolutely necessary
      • Generally avoid fancy new software that promises to make your life easier (it's a lie!)
      • Use computing to be efficient and powerful, not mindless and trendy
      • Or do you INVITE malware and viruses?




    4. Use pass phrases, not passwords. "2014 Olympian Gracie Gold is from Chatham!"
      • Adopt a personal eccentricity / discipline, such as doubling the last letter of each word:
      • "2014 Olympiann Graciee Goldd iss fromm Chathamm!"
      • Or do you want AI programs to guess your password?


    5. Write down your passwords but steganographically (Hide the passphrase among other text):
      • "No 2014 Olympian will know that my password is in this sentence, except Gracie Gold, who is from Chatham!"
      • Use your own character for spaces -- _, #, or even yy
      • Post it notes are actually good because you can't steal them electronically
      • Or do you like FORGETTING your passwords? Or GIVING them to whoever reads your post-it-notes?




    6. Designate a computer or laptop outside your firewall for docking USB devices
      • And treat USB devices like Starbucks coffee cups -- you can reuse them, but it's better to retire them
      • Use the cloud or email to transfer from that machine into your secure area
      • Actually they make nice data backups
      • Or do you want to PROPAGATE the next stuxnet?




    7. Reinstall your OS often
      • Think of it like washing your dog
      • Keep your data in one place if possible so reinstallation is easy
      • Keep your machine generic so reinstallation is easy
      • Reinstall when your machine slows strangely or has unusual constant network activity
      • Or do you like COHABITING with malware?




    8. Have multiple accounts, multiple devices, for low and high security activities
      • E.g., two gmail accounts, one for really important stuff
      • Or just business and personal accounts
      • Solves the co-worker / boss / four-star general / Senator / projector problem
      • Vary your personal information slightly so you can do forensics if there is a leak
      • Use some data as a honeypot for thieves
      • Or do you like it when others VIEW your inbox mail headers from your mom?




    9. Consider a Data Loss Prevention (DLP) Solution for your enterprise
      • It doesn't have to be expensive if you just check in software for dozens of SSNs on outbound packets
      • Sometimes it's easier to watch for intrusion than to prevent it












    A Final Plea From a Cyberwar Perspective





    • Cyberwarfare is already here: the next conflicts among major powers will be in cyberspace (and economic space)

      • That may not sound good, but it might be preferable to lobbing cruise missiles
      • And preferable to sending 100,000 men and women to faraway deserts


    • It's really Cyber Power, like economic sanctions and trade embargos (or blockades?)

    • It is not our job to make computer hardware and software perfectly secure

    • Some of the flaws in the systems will be exploited by US cyber warriors in the future

    • US Taxpayers should not be fixing overseas computer systems by making Microsoft and Google work for everyone

    • No one says it, but the reason the NSA does much of what it does is for fighting cyberwar, not just fighting terrorism

    • This may include securing the computer systems of multinational corporations with

      • Indian executives trained in the UK
      • Investor/Bankers in China
      • Markets in Brazil
      • and a workforce in Indonesia/Malaysia


    • However, if you want this country to remain strong:



      • YOU must secure your hardware and software as well as possible
      • DESPITE inherent design flaws and continuous discovery of exploits

      • YOU are the civilian cyber defenders
      • YOU are an important part of the country's infrastructure and defense of that infrastructure
      • YOU are a legitimate target of foreign attack

      • In a world of imperfect security,
      • S/HE WHO practices the BEST PRACTICES
      • will be the winner
      • or at least a survivor
